# Measuring-tape reference

Two high-resolution reference photos of the tape used in all 342 Bay
spot-checks. Purpose: orient anyone (incl. Claude) when interpreting
tape readings in flood photos — to confirm or push back against the
user's reported depth values rather than just take them at face value.

The user knows what the measurement is because they were there. These
notes exist so Claude has a fighting chance of independently verifying
from the photo. The user's reading is still authoritative; cross-check
is bonus.

## The tape

- Yellow steel tape, **25 ft total length** (the red "25FT" label
  inside the 4-in zone identifies the tape).
- Black hook (claw end) on the left in both reference photos. The
  hook is the reference point — distance is measured from the
  *inside* edge of the hook, i.e. the side facing the tape body. For
  the flood photos this end is laid down on / hooked over the **top
  of the grate** so the body of the tape extends downward into the
  grate slot toward the water.

## The most important rule for reading

**The line for the N-inch mark sits AFTER (to the right of) the
printed "N" digit, not at the digit's position.**

So if you see the digit "7" in a photo, the actual 7-inch line is the
long tick mark just past the "7" — closer to the "8" than to the "6".
This is the user's stated convention and matches the photos:

- In [`barnacle_measuring_tape - 2.jpeg`](barnacle_measuring_tape - 2.jpeg)
  (close-up of 0–4 in), the long inch-line tick sits to the **right
  edge** of each printed digit. The "2" sits in a small box, and the
  inch line is the long tick immediately after it (before the "FT &
  IN" label that occupies the 2-to-3 inch span).
- In [`barnacle_measuring_tape - 1.jpeg`](barnacle_measuring_tape - 1.jpeg)
  (full 0–13 in view), the same pattern holds for every digit 1
  through 11. The red "1F" foot marker after "11" sits at the 12-in
  position, with the line for 12 inches just after it.

If a tape reading looks ambiguous in a flood photo, default to the
line AFTER the digit, not centered on it.

## Subdivision lines (longest → shortest)

Between every pair of inch numbers, there are nested tick marks:

| Length | Spacing | What it marks |
|---|---|---|
| Longest | every 1 in | whole inch (the one just past the printed digit) |
| Next longest | every 1/2 in | half inch (centered between the inch ticks) |
| Medium | every 1/4 in | quarter inches |
| Short | every 1/8 in | eighths |
| Shortest | every 1/16 in | sixteenths (if the tape shows them) |

In flood photos, anything finer than 1/4 inch is usually
indistinguishable through water + camera angle. Don't claim a
sub-1/4-inch reading from a photo alone.

## Color cues on this tape

- **Red "25FT"** label inside the 4-inch zone — tape total length.
- **"FT & IN" / "INCHES"** label inside the 2-to-3-inch zone — just a
  units callout, not a measurement marker.
- **Red "1F"** at the 12-inch position — the 1-foot marker (each
  subsequent foot will be marked similarly: 2F at 24", 3F at 36", …).
- **Black tick marks and digits** on yellow background — the actual
  measurement scale.

## Why precision from photos is hard

The user warned: *"the 2D picture and the angle of the camera causes
crazy illusions. If I wasn't there, it would not be easy to get a
precise measurement from the pic alone."* True. Common failure modes:

- **Foreshortening**: tape held at an angle to camera plane compresses
  the visible scale. A "5 inch" gap in the photo might be 7 inches of
  actual tape.
- **Parallax through grate slots**: when the tape descends through a
  slot, the part underwater (refraction) shifts position vs. the part
  in air. Water line is the reference, not the tape body.
- **Tape sag**: even a steel tape sags a little when extended. Over a
  long drop the bottom of the tape may not be plumb.
- **Glare on the polished tape face** can hide tick marks at
  glancing angles.

The honest workflow is: read the tape from the photo, give a
±half-inch confidence band, and defer to the user's in-person reading
when they conflict.
